Risk category rules for animals and products of animal origin, from 31 January 2024

Published 7 December 2023

Applies to England, Scotland and Wales

This page outlines the rules that govern the risk categories for imported animals, products of animal origin (POAO) and animal byproducts, from 31 January 2024 – as set by the Border Target Operating Model (BTOM).  

There are 3 BTOM risk categories, each of which has specific rules that you need to follow before you can import your commodity from 31 January 2024:  

  • low risk  

  • medium risk  

  • high risk  

1.  What to do if your commodity is in the low risk category  

1.1  Required documentation  

You do not need a health certificate.  

However, low risk category consignments must come with a commercial document from the supplier.  

1.2  Notifying the British authorities  

Use the import of products, animals, food and feed system (IPAFFS) to notify authorities before the goods arrive in Great Britain.  

You will need the IPAFFS code, rather than the commodity code, to enter your details into IPAFFS. You can get the IPAFFS code from the tables that are included in the guidance.

1.3  Border checks  

From 30 April 2024 your goods must enter Great Britain through a point of entry with a border control post (BCP) that is designated to check your commodity.  

Low risk consignments will not be subject to routine documentary, identity and physical checks. However, from 30 April 2024 checks may still be undertaken where intelligence indicates a specific risk.  

1.4  EU goods coming through west coast ports  

For EU goods entering Great Britain through west coast ports, there will be a different implementation date for intelligence-led checks and for the requirement to enter through a port with a BCP. There is more detail in the Border Target Operating Model.  

2.  What to do if your commodity is in the medium risk category  

2.1  Required documentation  

From 31 January 2024 your consignment must have a health certificate issued by the competent authority in the country where the goods originate.  

2.2  Complete a health certificate

Find out how to complete a health certificate to export animals and animal products to Great Britain.    

2.3  Notifying the British authorities  

Use IPAFFS to notify authorities before the goods arrive in Great Britain.  

You will need the IPAFFS code, rather than the commodity code, to enter your details into IPAFFS. You can get the IPAFFS code from the tables that are included in the guidance.

2.4  Border checks  

From 30 April 2024 products in the medium risk category:  

  • will need to enter Great Britain through a border control post (BCP) that is designated to check your commodity  

  • may be subject to documentary, identity and physical import checks  

2.5  Goods coming through west coast ports  

For EU goods entering Great Britain through west coast ports, there will be a different implementation date for identity and physical checks and for the requirement to enter through a port with a BCP.   

There is more detail in the Border Target Operating Model.  

3.  What to do if your commodity is in the high risk category  

3.1  Required documentation  

Your commodity must have a health certificate issued by the competent authority in the country where the goods originate.  

3.2  Complete a health certificate

Find out how to complete a health certificate to export animals and animal products to Great Britain.  

3.3  Notifying the British authorities  

Use IPAFFS to notify authorities before the goods arrive in Great Britain.  

You will need the IPAFFS code, rather than the commodity code, to enter your details into IPAFFS. You can get the IPAFFS code from the tables that are included in the guidance.

3.4  Border checks  

Most consignments in the high TOM risk category are already subject to physical import checks. These checks will continue in the same way after 31 January 2024.  

4.  What to do if your commodity is in the medium or high risk categories, but there is no health certificate for your goods  

If a health certificate does not exist for the goods you want to import from the EU, you may need an import licence or authorisation.  

Contact the Animal and Plant Health Agency (APHA) Animal Imports team if:  

  • there’s no licence for your animal or germinal product  

  • you’re not sure if you need a licence  

If you are importing a medium-risk ABP, but there’s currently no health certificate for your goods, you will require a commercial document. 

5.  Get help  

If you have any questions about risk categories or how to import animals or animal products from 31 January 2024, email the APHA imports team at imports@apha.gov.uk.
